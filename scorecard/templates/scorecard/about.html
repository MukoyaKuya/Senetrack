ABOUT THE PROJECT

About SENETRACK

An independent civic-technology platform dedicated to making the Kenya Senate's work legible to every citizen.

Mission

Bring radical transparency to Kenya's Senate by converting raw Hansard transcripts, voting records, and attendance data into clear, comparable performance scores — so citizens can hold their senators accountable with evidence, not just opinion.

Who Built It

SENETRACK is built and maintained by a small team of civic-tech enthusiasts who believe open data and algorithmic accountability can strengthen Kenyan democracy.

We are not affiliated with, endorsed by, or funded by the Parliament of Kenya or any political party.

Contribute

SENETRACK is built in the open. Whether you are a developer, data journalist, or civic researcher, there are several ways to help:

  • Report a data error via GitHub Issues
  • Submit corrections to senator records
  • Challenge the methodology (see section 10)
  • Contribute to the Hansard parser & scraper

1 · Why We Use the Hansard

The Hansard is the official verbatim record of parliamentary debates and proceedings. In Kenya it provides a transparent, permanent, and machine-readable record of what every senator said, proposed, attended, and decided. No other single source offers the same combination of breadth, granularity, and official standing.

  • Transparency & accountability — Every speech, question, and vote is on record. Citizens can verify SENETRACK scores against the primary source.
  • Evidence of democratic debate — Hansard documents how issues are discussed and contested, showing whether deliberation is actually taking place.
  • Policy archive — Tracks how laws were debated before passage; researchers can study how positions evolved over time.
  • Civic education — Makes parliamentary work accessible to journalists, civil society, and the public.
  • Representation monitoring — Enables analysis of how senators represent their counties through questions, statements, and oversight actions.

Political scientists use Hansard for content analysis, discourse analysis, and voting-behaviour studies. SENETRACK uses it to quantify participation and debate in the Senate.

3 · The Hansard Engine — Exact Weights

The primary scoring engine (HansardEngine) processes Hansard 2025 data into a 0–100 score made up of three components. All weights below are the exact values used in production code.

Structural

55 pts

Voting · Attendance · Bills

Debate

45 pts

Words · Speeches · Motions

County + Bonus

5 + 3 pts

County representation · Statements bonus

3.1 Structural Pillar (55 pts)

Sub-component Points Formula
Voting Reliability25(votes_attended / votes_total) × 25
Plenary Attendance20(sessions_attended / 102) × 20
Bills Sponsored10log_score(bills, max=18) × 10 [+10% for nominated senators if bills > 0]

Hansard 2025 benchmarks used: max sessions = 102, max bills = 18. Log scaling formula: ln(1 + val) / ln(1 + max) × pts — caps "super-performers" so extreme outliers don't collapse the distribution.

3.2 Debate Pillar (45 pts)

Sub-component Points Formula
Speech Volume (words)20log_score(words, max=133,532) × 20
Speech Frequency15log_score(speeches, max=3,269) × 15
Motions Sponsored10log_score(motions, max=27) × 10

Hansard 2025 benchmarks: max words = 133,532, max speeches = 3,269, max motions = 27.

3.3 County Representation (5 pts) & Statements Bonus (up to 3 pts)

County pts = min(county_score, 10) / 10 × 5
Statements bonus = min(statements_2025, 30) / 30 × 3

The statements bonus rewards senators who submit formal statements (a quality-of-participation signal) but is capped at 3 pts so it cannot overpower attendance or debate scores.

Final Score = structural + debate + county_pts + statements_bonus
(theoretical max ≈ 108 before rounding; displayed on 0–100 scale)

5 · Legacy SPM Engine (fallback)

For senators without Hansard 2025 data (e.g. those who joined after the last import), SENETRACK falls back to the Weighted Multi-Factor Analysis (WMFA) engine. Its five-pillar weights are:

  • Parliamentary Participation — 30 pts (attendance 50% + speech intensity 50%)
  • Legislative Contributions — 20 pts (bills × 35 + passed × 25 + amendments × 10, normalised)
  • Committee Membership — 25 pts (role 40% + committee attendance 60% + 2 pt floor)
  • Voting Instance — 20 pts (votes attended / votes eligible × 20)
  • County Representation — 5 pts (county score 0–10 ÷ 2)

The legacy engine is used as a temporary measure only. Once Hansard data is available for a senator the HansardEngine takes over and their grade may change.

6 · Known Limitations

We publish these limitations openly so that scores are interpreted with appropriate caution.

Quantity rewarded over quality

Speech volume (words spoken) and frequency (number of speeches) are measurable from the Hansard; the impact or substance of a speech is not. A senator who delivers many short procedural interjections can score higher on the debate pillar than one who delivers fewer but landmark speeches. This is a structural limitation of any automated, Hansard-based system.

No qualitative analysis of bills or committee work

Sponsoring a bill counts the same regardless of whether it is a minor procedural motion or transformative legislation. Committee effectiveness is proxied by attendance and role, not by measurable policy outcomes. Full qualitative analysis would require human expert coding of legislative content, which is outside the current scope.

Data lag and import gaps

Scores reflect data available at the last import date. Senators who joined after the import cutoff, or whose Hansard records were incomplete at import time, may show "Still Computing" or lower scores that do not reflect recent activity.

Nominated senators structural disadvantage

Nominated senators do not represent a county and therefore automatically score 0 on the County Representation pillar. A small compensating boost is applied when bills are sponsored, but the structural bias remains and scores for nominated senators should be compared only within that cohort.

Voting Instance measures presence, not position

The voting sub-score rewards showing up to vote. It does not assess whether the senator voted in citizens' interests, followed party line, or voted independently. Abstentions are treated as presence (senator showed up), not absence.

7 · Disclaimers

Not an official government product

SENETRACK is an independent civic-technology platform. It is not affiliated with, endorsed by, or produced by the Parliament of Kenya, the Senate, or any government body.

Scores are indicative, not definitive

SPM scores are algorithmic proxies for measurable parliamentary activity. They do not constitute a comprehensive or final judgement of a senator's effectiveness, character, or contribution to public life.

Data accuracy depends on source records

SENETRACK imports data from Parliament of Kenya Hansard records and Mzalendo. If those source records contain errors or omissions, the scores will reflect those errors. We cannot independently verify every Hansard entry.

No legal or electoral use

SPM scores should not be used as the sole basis for any legal, regulatory, or formal electoral process. They are published for public information and civic engagement only.

Methodology evolves

Weights, benchmarks, and engine logic may change between data cycles as better data or research becomes available. Material changes will be documented on this page with version notes.
