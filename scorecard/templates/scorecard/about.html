Senator Performance Methodology

This scorecard applies a documented, data-driven methodology to transform raw parliamentary records into a single 0–100 performance score for each senator. The approach follows standard practice in quantitative political science: clearly defined indicators, transparent weights, and explicit formulas.

Why We Use the Hansard

In political science, the Hansard is the official verbatim record of debates and proceedings in Parliament. In Kenya, Hansard provides a transparent and permanent record of what legislators say, propose, and decide in institutions like the Parliament of Kenya.

Why Hansard matters for Kenya’s progressive democracy

  • Transparency and accountability — Hansard records every speech, question, and response made by Members of Parliament. Citizens, researchers, and civil society can review it to hold leaders accountable for their statements and commitments.
  • Evidence of democratic debate — A progressive democracy relies on open debate. Hansard documents how issues are discussed, contested, and negotiated in Parliament, showing whether democratic deliberation is taking place.
  • Historical and policy record — It serves as an archive of legislative history, tracking how laws were debated before being passed. Scholars can analyze how policies evolved over time.
  • Public participation and civic education — By making parliamentary debates accessible, Hansard enables citizens, journalists, and organizations to understand government decisions and participate more effectively in democratic processes.
  • Monitoring representation — Researchers can analyze Hansard to evaluate how effectively MPs represent their constituencies: what issues they raise and how often they participate in debates.

In simple terms: Hansard helps measure how open, accountable, and participatory Kenya’s democracy is, because it provides a detailed public record of parliamentary decision-making and debate.

Political scientists use Hansard data in many ways—content analysis, discourse analysis, voting behavior, and more—to measure democratic progress. This scorecard uses it to quantify participation and debate in the Senate.

1. Data Sources and Coverage

The engine draws on administrative and parliamentary records only. No opinion polls, surveys, or manual “expert scores” are used.

  • Hansard transcripts: word-level records of plenary debates and interventions.
  • Attendance registers: official sitting-by-sitting attendance for plenary and committees.
  • Legislative activity: sponsored bills, passed bills, and amendments credited to each senator.
  • Committee records: membership, leadership roles, and attendance per committee sitting.
  • Division lists: how each senator voted (or if they were absent) when a formal vote was recorded.
  • County-level indicators: a composite score reflecting constituency-focused activity and county development engagement.

Where an indicator is missing for a senator (for example, for a newly sworn-in member), the model withholds the final grade and displays “NEW” until a minimum evidence threshold is met rather than guessing a score.

2. From Raw Activity to Comparable Indicators

For each senator we construct a set of raw indicators. Let:

  • A = plenary attendance rate (0–1), based on days present / days eligible.
  • S = total number of substantive speeches recorded in the Hansard.
  • Bs = number of bills sponsored; Bp = number of bills passed.
  • M = number of formal amendments moved.
  • Ca = committee attendance rate (0–1).
  • R = committee role score (0 for member, > 0 for chair/vice-chair, scaled).
  • Vatt = votes attended; Vtot = votes where the senator was eligible.
  • Cc = county representation score (0–10) from constituency-linked activity.

To make these indicators comparable across senators, we apply a combination of rate-based measures and logarithmic scaling for highly skewed counts (such as speeches):

speech_intensity = ln(1 + S) / ln(1 + S95)

where S95 is the 95th percentile number of speeches across all senators. This caps “super-speakers” so that one or two very active members do not dominate the scale, a standard approach in productivity analysis.

3. The Five Public Pillars and Weights

Internally, the engine works with many sub-scores. For communication with the public, these are summarised into five pillars that together sum to 100 points:

  • Participation — 30 points (30%)
  • Legislative — 20 points (20%)
  • Committee — 25 points (25%)
  • Voting — 20 points (20%)
  • County Representation — 5 points (5%)

Each pillar is first expressed on a 0–1 scale, then mapped into its share of the 100-point total. Denote the pillar-level scores by P, L, C, V, and R (all between 0 and 1). The overall score is:

Total Score = 100 × (0.30·P + 0.20·L + 0.25·C + 0.20·V + 0.05·R)

3.1 Participation (30 points)

Participation combines attendance and substantive plenary contribution:

P = 0.5·A + 0.5·speech_intensity

This balances “showing up” and “speaking up”. A senator who is always present but never speaks, or who speaks often but rarely attends, will not achieve a high participation score.

3.2 Legislative Work (20 points)

Legislative work rewards quality-adjusted output: sponsoring bills, seeing them pass, and moving amendments. A simple linear index is constructed and then normalised to 0–1:

raw_legislative = 35·Bs + 25·Bp + 10·M
L = raw_legislative / max(raw_legislative across senators)

Passed bills count more than merely sponsored ones; amendments are valued but at a lower weight, reflecting typical practice in legislative productivity studies.

3.3 Committee Work (25 points)

Committees are where most detailed legislative work happens. The committee pillar combines leadership roles and attendance:

C = 0.40·R + 0.60·Ca + 0.02

The constant term (0.02) avoids giving extremely low or zero scores to members of small or newly formed committees, a standard technique to stabilise indices in small-sample settings.

3.4 Voting Discipline (20 points)

Voting discipline measures whether the senator is present when decisions are formally taken:

V = Vatt / Vtot

Only votes where the senator was eligible are counted. Abstentions recorded in the division lists are distinguished from absence and treated as presence.

3.5 County Representation (5 points)

County representation focuses on constituency-linked activity and engagement, scaled from 0–10 and mapped onto the 5-point share of the total:

R = Cc / 10
